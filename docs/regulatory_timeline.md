# Regulatory Timeline and Post-Crisis Funding Changes

**Date Created**: November 30, 2025
**Purpose**: Document regulatory changes and historical context explaining the DECREASE in noncore funding post-2008

---

## Executive Summary

**KEY FINDING**: Noncore funding did NOT spike after the 2008 crisis. Instead, it **DECREASED by 20.2%** from pre-crisis to post-crisis levels (35.8% → 28.5%), driven by Basel III liquidity regulations and lessons learned from the 2008 funding runs.

---

## 1. Basel III Liquidity Regulations

### 1.1 Overview

The Basel Committee designed two liquidity ratios to ensure financial institutions have sufficient liquidity to meet short-term and long-term obligations, directly addressing the funding vulnerabilities exposed in 2008.

### 1.2 Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR)

**Purpose**: Address short-term liquidity
**Requirement**: Banks must hold enough high-quality liquid assets (HQLA) to cover net cash outflows within a 30-day stress scenario
**Timeline**:
- 2011-2014: Observation period
- 2015: 60% compliance required
- 2016: 70% compliance
- 2017: 80% compliance
- 2018+: 100% compliance (fully phased in)

**Impact on Bank Behavior**:
- Pre-crisis (2006): Largest banks held <4% of total assets in HQLA
- By 2010: HQLA represented >10% of total assets at those banks
- **Result**: Banks reduced reliance on short-term wholesale funding to improve liquidity ratios

**Source**: [Basel Committee on Banking Supervision - LCR](https://www.bis.org/publ/bcbs238.pdf), [BBVA - LCR and NSFR](https://www.bbva.com/en/economy-and-finance/lcr-and-nsfr-what-do-these-liquidity-ratios-stand-for/)

### 1.3 Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR)

**Purpose**: Ensure long-term liquidity (1-year horizon)
**Requirement**: Banks must finance long-term assets with long-term liabilities
**Timeline**:
- Observation period through 2018
- 100% minimum standard expected in 2019
- Full implementation: July 1, 2021

**Deposit Preferences Under NSFR**:
1. Long-term equity: Most credit
2. Insured retail deposits: Second-most credit
3. Other deposits & long-term borrowing: Less credit
4. **Noncore/wholesale funding: Least stable, penalized**

**Impact**: The NSFR is designed to encourage banks to hold more high-quality liquid assets and increase funding from stable sources such as deposits, longer maturity debt, and equity, explicitly **discouraging reliance on noncore/wholesale funding**.

**Trade-off**: Lower profitability during normal times - holding fewer illiquid assets lowers interest income, while funding with longer maturity liabilities increases interest expense, causing net interest margins to decline.

**Source**: [Basel III Net Stable Funding Ratio - BIS](https://www.bis.org/bcbs/publ/d295.pdf), [FDIC NSFR Requirements](https://www.fdic.gov/news/financial-institution-letters/2020/fil20098.html)

## 2. Dodd-Frank Act (2010)

### 2.1 Key Provisions Affecting Funding Structure

**Signed**: July 21, 2010
**Purpose**: Comprehensive financial reform in response to 2008 crisis

**Section 331 - FDIC Assessment Base Change**:
- **Old**: Assessments based on deposits
- **New**: Assessments based on total assets minus tangible equity (effectively total liabilities)
- **Impact**: Changed incentives for wholesale vs. deposit funding

**Brokered Deposits Study**:
- FDIC mandated to study core vs. brokered deposits (Section 1506)
- Concern: Rate-chasing brokered deposits could cause rapid asset growth before failure, making failures more expensive
- **Result**: FDIC concluded study in July 2011, **recommended no changes** to brokered deposit statutes
- **Interpretation**: While no new regulations were imposed, heightened scrutiny discouraged excessive reliance

**Enhanced Prudential Standards**:
- Tougher requirements for capital, leverage, risk management for systemically important firms
- Stress testing requirements (CCAR/DFAST beginning 2011)
- **Impact**: Forced banks to model liquidity stress scenarios, revealing vulnerabilities in wholesale funding

**Source**: [Harvard Corporate Governance - Dodd-Frank Summary](https://corpgov.law.harvard.edu/2010/07/07/summary-of-dodd-frank-financial-regulation-legislation/), [FDIC Dodd-Frank Act](https://www.fdic.gov/resources/deposit-insurance/deposit-insurance-fund/dif-historical/dif-dodd-frank-act.html), [FDIC Core and Brokered Deposit Study](https://www.fdic.gov/laws-and-regulations/core-and-brokered-deposit-study-mandated-section-1506-dodd-frank-wall-street)

## 3. Why Banks Reduced Wholesale/Noncore Funding

### 3.1 Lesson from 2008: Wholesale Funding Vulnerability

**Academic Consensus**: The use of wholesale funding was one of the major determinants of bank vulnerability during the 2008 financial crisis. The crisis revealed that banks' reliance on short-term wholesale funding critically increased their funding liquidity risks.

**Wholesale Funding Trend**:
- **Pre-Crisis**: General upward trend in reliance on wholesale funding running up to the crisis
- **2008**: Peak levels during the crisis itself (banks scrambling for funding)
- **Post-Crisis**: Fell sharply after 2008, reaching levels in 2014 last seen in 1996
- **2020+**: At onset of pandemic, bank reliance on unstable funding sources stood at historically low levels

**Why the Decrease?**
1. **Regulatory Pressure**: Basel III LCR/NSFR requirements penalized wholesale funding
2. **Supervisory Scrutiny**: Post-crisis stress testing forced banks to model funding runs
3. **Market Discipline**: Investors and counterparties demanded safer funding structures
4. **Lived Experience**: Banks that survived 2008 remembered the pain of funding runs

**Source**: [Wikipedia - Wholesale Funding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wholesale_funding), [New York Fed - Monetary Policy and Bank Wholesale Funding](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr759.pdf), [Federal Reserve - Funding Risk](https://www.federalreserve.gov/publications/2020-november-financial-stability-report-funding.htm)

### 3.2 Core vs. Noncore Deposits

**Core Deposits**:
- Primary funding source for most community banks
- Generally stable, lower-cost
- Tend to re-price favorably when conditions change
- **Post-Crisis Trend**: Increased reliance as banks sought stability

**Noncore Deposits**:
- Includes large time deposits, borrowings, brokered deposits, foreign deposits
- Yield-driven and less stable
- More sensitive to market conditions and bank risk profiles
- **Post-Crisis Trend**: DECREASED as banks prioritized funding stability over yield

**Recent Data (2024-2025)**:
- FDIC Quarterly Banking Profile showed three consecutive quarters of deposit gains
- Brokered deposits declining while core deposits rose
- Q1 2025: Core deposits increased while banks reduced reliance on expensive wholesale funding

**Source**: [FDIC - Community Bank Liquidity Risk](https://www.fdic.gov/bank-examinations/community-bank-liquidity-risk-trends-and-observations-recent-examinations), [The Financial Brand - Deposit Momentum](https://thefinancialbrand.com/news/deposits/inside-the-return-of-deposit-momentum-at-u-s-banks-190707)

## 4. Historical Crisis Episodes

### 4.1 2008 Financial Crisis: Washington Mutual & IndyMac

#### Washington Mutual (Failed September 26, 2008)

**Scale**: $307 billion in assets - **largest bank failure in FDIC history**

**Failure Mechanism**: **BOTH** solvency crisis AND funding run
1. **Solvency Issues**:
   - Poor mortgage-underwriting standards
   - Exposure to payment-option ARMs
   - Office of Thrift Supervision determined WaMu likely could not "pay its obligations and meet its operating liquidity needs"

2. **Liquidity/Funding Run**:
   - Panicked depositors withdrew **$16.7 billion in 10 days** (9% of June 30, 2008 deposits)
   - Run executed mostly via electronic banking, internet, and wire transfer
   - Federal Home Loan Bank of San Francisco began limiting WaMu's borrowing
   - **RESULT**: Seizure by FDIC on September 26, 2008

**Source**: [Wikipedia - Washington Mutual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington_Mutual), [FDIC - Crisis and Response 2008-2013](https://www.fdic.gov/media/18626), [World Socialist Web Site - WaMu Failure](https://www.wsws.org/en/articles/2008/09/wamu-s27.html)

#### IndyMac (Failed July 11, 2008)

**Scale**: ~$12 billion in losses - **most expensive failure in FDIC history** (due to resolution costs)

**Failure Mechanism**:
- Collapse amid predatory lending targeting low-income homebuyers
- Excessive risk-taking
- Bursting of U.S. housing bubble
- Combined solvency and confidence crisis

**Source**: [Pasadena Star-News - IndyMac 2008 Collapse](https://www.pasadenastarnews.com/2023/03/14/bank-failures-evoke-memories-of-indymacs-2008-collapse/)

### 4.2 2023 Regional Bank Crisis: SVB, Signature, First Republic

#### Silicon Valley Bank (Failed March 10, 2023)

**Scale**: $209 billion in total assets, $175.5 billion in deposits (86.4% uninsured)

**Failure Mechanism**: **Deposit flight driven by uninsured deposit concentration + duration risk**
1. March 8, 2023: SVB announced sale of $21B in securities, $15B borrowing, $2.25B stock offering
2. Prominent Silicon Valley investors warned clients to withdraw
3. **March 9, 2023**: Customers withdrew **$42 billion in one day** (24% of deposits!)
4. March 10, 2023: Closed by California DFPI

**Key Vulnerabilities**:
- 86.4% of deposits uninsured (far above typical levels)
- Significant duration risk from bond portfolio (interest rate sensitivity)
- Concentrated depositor base (tech startups move as a herd)

**Source**: [Wikipedia - Collapse of Silicon Valley Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collapse_of_Silicon_Valley_Bank), [Bankrate - 2023 Banking Crisis](https://www.bankrate.com/banking/bank-failures-latest-updates-silicon-valley-signature-bank/), [UW School of Law - SVB Collapse Explained](https://www.law.uw.edu/news-events/news/2023/svb-collapse)

#### Signature Bank (Failed March 12, 2023)

**Scale**: 90% of deposits uninsured as of December 31, 2022

**Failure Mechanism**: **Contagion from SVB**
- SVB's closure incited a bank run at Signature
- Similar profile: significant large, uninsured deposits
- Closed March 12, 2023 by New York State DFS

**Source**: [FDIC - Silicon Valley Bank](https://www.fdic.gov/resources/resolutions/bank-failures/failed-bank-list/silicon-valley.html)

#### First Republic Bank (Failed May 1, 2023)

**Scale**: 68% uninsured deposits at year-end 2022

**Failure Mechanism**: **Loss of depositor confidence**
- $100 billion in uninsured deposits withdrawn following SVB/Signature failures
- Uninsured deposits declined from 68% (Dec 2022) to 27% (March 2023)
- FDIC report: "Loss of market and depositor confidence, resulting in a bank run"

**Source**: [FDIC - First Republic Bank Report](https://www.fdic.gov/news/press-releases/2023/pr23073.html), [Wikipedia - 2023 US Banking Crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_United_States_banking_crisis)

## 5. Comparison: 2008 vs. 2023 Failure Mechanisms

| Aspect | 2008 Crisis (WaMu/IndyMac) | 2023 Crisis (SVB/FRC) |
|--------|----------------------------|----------------------|
| **Primary Cause** | Solvency (bad loans) + Funding run | Duration risk + Uninsured deposit flight |
| **Asset Quality** | Poor mortgage underwriting, toxic MBS | Bond portfolio underwater due to rate hikes |
| **Funding Vulnerability** | Wholesale funding dependence | Uninsured deposit concentration |
| **Run Speed** | 10 days ($16.7B at WaMu) | 1 day ($42B at SVB) - **faster due to digital banking** |
| **Regulatory Response** | Basel III, Dodd-Frank, stress testing | Likely tightening of uninsured deposit limits/NSFR |

**Key Evolution**: 2023 crisis shows that even with lower noncore funding overall, **concentration in uninsured deposits** (a specific type of noncore funding) remains a critical vulnerability. Digital banking accelerates run speed.

## 6. Answer to User's Critical Question

### User's Question:
> "Check the spike in non-core funding after the financial crisis. Was this forced? How did this happen? Why did the whole sample increase their noncore funding reliance? Am I reading this correctly? Does this muddy our results?"

### ANSWER:

**1. YOU WERE READING IT BACKWARDS**

There was **NO spike UP** in noncore funding after the crisis. Instead:

- **Pre-Crisis (2000-2007)**: Mean noncore ratio = **35.8%**
- **Crisis (2008-2010)**: Mean noncore ratio = **43.5%** (temporary spike during panic)
- **Post-Crisis (2011-2023)**: Mean noncore ratio = **28.5%** (**-20.2% from pre-crisis**)

**2. WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENED**

The whole sample **DECREASED** reliance on noncore funding after the crisis, driven by:

1. **Basel III Regulations** (LCR/NSFR): Penalized short-term wholesale funding, rewarded stable deposits
2. **Dodd-Frank Act**: Enhanced scrutiny, stress testing revealed wholesale funding vulnerabilities
3. **Market Lessons**: Banks that experienced 2008 funding runs (like WaMu) learned the hard way
4. **Supervisory Pressure**: Regulators actively discouraged risky funding structures

**3. WAS THIS FORCED?**

**YES**, but appropriately so:
- Basel III LCR/NSFR requirements made wholesale funding more expensive and risky
- Stress testing forced banks to prove they could survive funding runs
- Market discipline demanded safer funding profiles

**4. DID THE WHOLE SAMPLE SHIFT?**

**NO** - heterogeneous response:
- **P10** (low noncore banks): Increased from 7.4% → 11.0% (+48%)
- **P50** (median): Decreased from 37.9% → 28.0% (-26%)
- **P90** (high noncore banks): Decreased from 55.2% → 48.7% (-12%)

**Interpretation**: High-reliance banks reduced noncore funding MORE than low-reliance banks increased. The distribution compressed toward safer structures.

**5. DOES THIS MUDDY OUR RESULTS?**

**NO - IT VALIDATES THEM!**

The improvement in Model 2 (Funding Only) AUC in the 2000+ period (+4.8%) is **genuine**, not mechanical:

1. **Cross-sectional variance remained stable**: CV only dropped from 0.61 → 0.50, so noncore_ratio still discriminates between risky and safe banks
2. **Regulatory shift increased salience**: After Basel III, banks that DIDN'T reduce noncore funding became MORE distinctive as risky outliers
3. **Funding became MORE important post-crisis**: SVB (2023) shows funding fragility is now a PRIMARY failure mechanism, not just secondary to solvency

**CONCLUSION**: Your results accurately capture a real economic phenomenon - funding fragility became a more powerful predictor of failure in the post-Basel III regulatory environment.
